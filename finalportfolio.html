Major Project 1

Job Portfolio

Context

This project required the creation of a professional application package tailored to a specific company and role. I chose to target Bloomberg for their Software Engineering & Quantitative Analytics Internship.

Rhetorical Strategy

My primary goal was to shift away from a chronological list of achievements and instead construct a central narrative of problem-solving. In my cover letter, I utilized a narrative structure, connecting my experience in the Bloomberg Bpuzzled Global Finals to my technical work at athenaHealth. This allowed me to frame my qualifications not just as technical skills, but as a demonstration of analytical rigor and high-pressure problem-solving. This portfolio demonstrates my ability to align personal branding with the specific values (speed, scale, reliability) of a target organization.

Major Project 2

Technical Communication Crisis Poster

Context

For this project, we researched the 2018 Google Location Tracking Controversy, a "crisis of discovery" where Google continued to track user location even after the "Location History" setting was disabled.

Rhetorical Strategy

We developed a research poster titled "Deception by Design" to analyze the ethical failures behind Google's "Dark Patterns." My part of the rhetorical strategy focused on visual evidence: I created a comparative breakdown of the "Fragmented Interface" (Location History vs. Web & App Activity) to visually demonstrate to the audience exactly how the deception occurred. Additionally, I utilized a bar chart comparing "Major Financial Settlements" (peaking at the $391.5M coalition settlement) to quantify the real-world business consequences of unethical technical communication.

Major Project 3

Community Technical Communication Toolkit

Context

I partnered with Grey Hat GT, Georgia Tech's cybersecurity club, to address a critical "Accessibility Gap" in their operations. While the club claimed to be "Open to Everyone," their digital infrastructure lacked technical accessibility.

Rhetorical Strategy

I created a dual-pronged toolkit: a Recruitment Brochure and a Web Accessibility SOP. The brochure utilizes a "Curiosity to Career" rhetorical structure and data visualization (pie charts) to demystify cybersecurity for non-CS majors. Conversely, the SOP uses high-technical specificity and imperative language to ensure future web teams maintain WCAG compliance. This project demonstrates my ability to adapt tone and format—visual persuasion for recruits vs. Markdown documentation for developers—to serve a single organizational mission.

Final Argument

Self-Assessment Analysis

From Code to Connection: Bridging the Technical Gap

Grade Argument: A

Introduction

At the beginning of this semester, I wrote to you about a specific failure I experienced during my internship at athenaHealth. I had built a functioning neural network, but I "completely fumbled" the presentation because I focused on technical details rather than business impact. My goal for LMC 3403 was to learn how to "translate technical ideas" for diverse audiences. Looking back at my portfolio, I argue that I have earned an A in this course. I have not only met my initial goal of bridging the gap between research and public understanding but have also demonstrated mastery of the course outcomes: Rhetoric, Process, Modes & Media, and Design.

Outcome 1: Rhetoric

I have learned that effective communication requires meeting the audience "where they are." This is most evident in Major Project 3. For the Grey Hat GT brochure, I faced an audience intimidated by cybersecurity. I employed a rhetorical strategy of "Visual Evidence," using a demographic pie chart to prove that 55% of members come from non-CS backgrounds. This directly countered the audience's assumption of exclusivity.

In contrast, for Major Project 1, my audience was the Bloomberg recruiting team. Here, I shifted my rhetorical approach from "listing accomplishments" to "narrative storytelling." By framing my resume through the lens of the Bloomberg Bpuzzled competition, I created a "personal connection" to the company culture, demonstrating that I understand not just what they do, but how they think.

Outcome 2: Process

My work this semester was defined by iterative revision. In Major Project 1, I initially focused on a general software role. However, after analyzing my SMART goals, I realized I needed to be more targeted. I "completely changed my focus" in the cover letter to align with my grad school and career ambitions, proving that I view communication as an evolving process rather than a static product.

Similarly, in Major Project 3, I recognized a "scalability" issue in my initial design. I recommended updating the "Top 5 CTF Topics" bar chart annually so the brochure would not become obsolete. This foresight demonstrates that I am thinking about the lifecycle of a document, not just its immediate submission.

Outcome 3: Modes & Media

I have demonstrated the ability to select the correct medium for the message. In Major Project 3, I utilized two distinct modes for two distinct audiences:

  • Print/Visual (Brochure): I used a tri-fold format with QR codes to facilitate immediate physical-to-digital conversion during club fairs.
  • Code/Documentation (SOP): I recognized that a PDF would be useless to a developer. Instead, I wrote the Standard Operating Procedure in Markdown (.md) to live directly in the club's GitHub repository. This ensured the documentation fit the "existing GitHub workflow" of the technical team.

Outcome 4: Design

My design skills have grown from simple formatting to intentional visual hierarchy and data visualization. In Major Project 2, I used design to simplify complex legal arguments. By creating a bar chart of the $391.5M Multi-State Settlement, I allowed the viewer to instantly grasp the magnitude of Google's failure without reading the fine print. I also applied these accessibility principles to this portfolio website itself, ensuring high-contrast text and semantic HTML structure.

Similarly, in the Grey Hat Brochure, I strictly adhered to the "Navy Blue and Gold" brand identity to ensure institutional recognition. I structured the layout to mimic a student's thought process: Definition → Action → Justification → Proof. By using "We Get Hired" sections with employer logos (Google, NSA), I used design to provide social proof of the club's value.

Conclusion

This semester, I transformed from a student who focused solely on "accuracy" to a technical communicator who prioritizes impact. I have analyzed audiences (Rhetoric), refined my work through feedback (Process), navigated between GitHub and Print (Modes), and created professional visual identities (Design). Because I have submitted a complete portfolio, actively engaged in all Career Blogs, and met the attendance requirements, I confidently submit this portfolio for a final grade of A.
